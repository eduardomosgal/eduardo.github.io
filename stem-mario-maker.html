STEM Mario Maker

An educational ecosystem for children with Down Syndrome (ages 9-13) that teaches mechanical principles through multisensory play.

Context

STEM Mario Maker is a multisensory tool designed for children with Down Syndrome. It bridges the gap between digital puzzles in an app and physical mechanical movement. By solving challenges about "how things move," users unlock the power to activate a physical toy, reinforcing cognitive links through immediate reward.

The Challenge

The main hurdle was translating abstract geometry into tangible mechanics. We needed to ensure that children (ages 9-13) could understand that changing a physical part (the biela) directly affected the character's movement, all while keeping the system lag-free and the interaction physically accessible for their motor skills.

The Goal & My Role

I led the technical architecture, ensuring that the software logic and the physical mechanisms worked as a single cohesive unit.

  • ⚡ Mechanical Design: Implementing Slider-Crank (Biela-Manivela) mechanisms.
  • 💻 Embedded Logic: Programming the Arduino core to handle Game States and app-sync.
  • 🛠️ Multisensory UX: Synchronizing DC Motors, RGB LEDs, and auditory feedback.
  • 📦 Fabrication: Laser cut, 3D printing and post-processing of the modular components.

Hardware Integration

Stable communication between the Arduino core and the app was critical. I implemented a robust power distribution system to handle the high current draw of the DC motor without affecting the sensor stability.

Motor and slider-crank mechanism in action.

Outcome

A functional STEM tool that proves how physical feedback—specifically mechanical motion—drives engagement and understanding for children with Down Syndrome more effectively than digital-only rewards.

Reflection

This project taught me that inclusive design isn't about simplifying content, but about diversifying the way we provide feedback. For a child with Down Syndrome, the physical vibration of a motor and the sight of a moving gear provide a much stronger "success" cue than any screen icon ever could. Precise timing in physical systems is the foundation of digital-physical immersion.
